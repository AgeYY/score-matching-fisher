\section{Estimating Hellinger distance RDM using Flow Matching}

In previous section, we showed that denoising score matching method is able to estimate the Hellinger distance well (Figure \ref{fig:rsa_nested_subset_convergence}). The dataset we used is, however, a simple line manifold in a two dimensional space. Here we test our method using a high dimensional toy dataset.

\subsection{Toy dataset}

We use a synthetic conditional model for a scalar stimulus $\theta$ and a multivariate observation $x \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ with $d \ge 2$.
Stimuli are drawn independently from a uniform density on a bounded interval,
\begin{equation}
  \theta \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(\theta_{\mathrm{low}}, \theta_{\mathrm{high}}).
\end{equation}
For each neuron $j \in \{1,\ldots,d\}$ we fix a \emph{tuning center} $\theta_j$ by placing $d$ equally spaced points on $[\theta_{\mathrm{low}}, \theta_{\mathrm{high}}]$ (endpoints included).

\paragraph{Random bump amplitudes.}
Before drawing joint samples $(\theta, x)$, we sample one amplitude per neuron,
\begin{equation}
  a_j \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(a_{\mathrm{low}}, a_{\mathrm{high}}),
  \qquad j = 1,\ldots,d,
\end{equation}
independently across $j$.
The vector $(a_1,\ldots,a_d)$ is then held fixed.

\paragraph{Conditional mean (tuning curve).}
Given $\theta$, the mean of $x_j$ is a Gaussian bump in $\theta$ centered at $\theta_j$:
\begin{equation}
  \mu_j(\theta) = a_j \exp\!\bigl(-\kappa\,\bigl(\omega\,(\theta - \theta_j)\bigr)^2\bigr),
  \qquad j = 1,\ldots,d,
\end{equation}
with bump precision $\kappa \ge 0$ and stimulus-axis scale $\omega$.
The conditional mean vector is $\mu(\theta) = (\mu_1(\theta),\ldots,\mu_d(\theta))^{\mathsf{T}}$.

\paragraph{Observation noise.}
Conditionally on $\theta$, the observation is multivariate Gaussian with diagonal covariance.
Let $\sigma > 0$ be a constant baseline noise scale shared across all neurons, and let $\alpha$ be a fixed activity--variance coupling strength (the arithmetic mean of two nonnegative hyperparameters in the implementation).
The per-neuron conditional variances are
\begin{equation}
  v_j(\theta) = d\,\sigma^{2}\,\bigl(1 + \alpha\,|\mu_j(\theta)|\bigr),
  \qquad j = 1,\ldots,d,
\end{equation}
with independence across neurons,
\begin{equation}
  x \mid \theta \sim \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\mu(\theta),\,\mathrm{diag}(v_1(\theta),\ldots,v_d(\theta))\bigr).
\end{equation}
Thus noise magnitude increases with the absolute tuned response $|\mu_j(\theta)|$, and each neuron's noise standard deviation scales proportionally to $\sqrt{d}$ for fixed $\sigma$, $\alpha$, and $\mu_j(\theta)$.
The leading factor~$d$ ties per-neuron variance to the population size~$d$: as more neurons are observed, each carries proportionally larger variance so that increasing dimension alone does not drive the joint observation toward an effectively noiseless, trivially informative regime for~$\theta$.

\subsection{Ground truth RDM computation}

\paragraph{Hellinger distance.}
We discretize the stimulus range into $B$ bins. For bins $i,j$, write $p_i(x)=p(x\mid\bar{\theta}_i)$ and $p_j(x)=p(x\mid\bar{\theta}_j)$ for the conditional observation density implied by the toy generative model (Gaussian mean $\mu(\theta)$ and diagonal variances $v_j(\theta)$ as above). We estimate the squared Hellinger $H^2_{ij}$ using the one-sided identity:
\begin{equation}
  H^2_{ij}
  = 1 - \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p_i}\!\left[
    \exp\!\Bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(\log p_j(x)-\log p_i(x)\bigr)\Bigr)
  \right],
\end{equation}
and the expectation is replaced by a sample mean. Specifically, draw $m$ samples from $p(\cdot\mid\bar{\theta}_i)$ for each row~$i$, evaluate $\log p(x\mid\bar{\theta}_i)$ and $\log p(x\mid\bar{\theta}_j)$ under the closed-form Gaussian likelihood, and average the exponentials (implemented with a stabilized log-mean-exp). The result is clipped to $[0,1]$. The squared-root of this $B \times B$ matrix is the ground truth Hellinger distance matrix. Non-diagonal entries are used for computing correlation with the estimated RDM.

\paragraph{Pairwise decoding.}
The GT of pairwise decoding is estimated by binning the data and using logistic regression to estimate decoding accuracy with large $n_{\mathrm{ref}} = 5000$ datapoints.

\subsection{Denoising score matching does not work very well for high-dimensional dataset}

In two dimensions, the learned Hellinger RDM tracks the Monte Carlo target closely as the nested training size~$n$ increases, outperforming the binning-based decoding baseline (Figure \ref{fig:high_d_dsm}).
At $d=100$, however, neither the DSM-based Hellinger estimate nor the binned decoding matrix matches to approximated GT well at comparable~$n$. I also tried 50D example, the result is similar, both method does not work very well. 

In particular, for $d=100$ the DSM estimate exhibits a false signal of artificially low squared region in the middle of the matrix, which raises concerns for practical use of the method in high dimension.

\subsection{Flow matching: a short introduction}

Flow matching learns a time-dependent vector field that transports samples from a simple base distribution to a target distribution by integrating an ordinary differential equation (ODE).
Let $t \in [0,1]$ and let $p_0$ denote a tractable reference on $\mathbb{R}^{d}$ (for example, a standard Gaussian).
The goal is to construct a velocity field $v_{\phi}(x,t)$ such that trajectories solving
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} x_t = v_{\phi}(x_t,t), \qquad x_0 \sim p_0,
\end{equation}
induce $x_1$ to be close to a target $p_1$ (in our case, the model distribution or a conditional $p_1(\cdot\mid\theta)$).

To obtain the regression objective of velocity field, one needs to first define a probability path from $x_0$ to $x_1$. A concrete construction is affine path. Fix scalar functions $\alpha,\beta : [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}$ with $\alpha(0)=1$, $\beta(0)=0$, $\alpha(1)=0$, and $\beta(1)=1$, and define the affine path
\begin{equation}
  x_t = \alpha(t)\,x_0 + \beta(t)\,x_1,
  \label{eq:flow_affine_bridge}
\end{equation}
so that $x_t=x_0$ at $t=0$ and $x_t=x_1$ at $t=1$.
Holding $(x_0,x_1)$ fixed, the induced velocity is
\begin{equation}
  u_t := \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} x_t = \alpha'(t)\,x_0 + \beta'(t)\,x_1.
  \label{eq:flow_affine_velocity}
\end{equation}
Given $t$ sampled uniformly on $[0,1]$, a simple Monte Carlo flow-matching objective matches the network prediction to this bridge velocity:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FM}}(\phi)
  =
  \mathbb{E}_{t \sim \mathcal{U}[0,1]}\,
  \mathbb{E}_{x_0 \sim p_0,\; x_1 \sim p_1}
  \Bigl[
    \bigl\lVert v_{\phi}(x_t,t) - u_t \bigr\rVert_2^2
  \Bigr].
\end{equation}
Minimizing $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FM}}$ encourages $v_{\phi}$ to realize the same time evolution as the chosen coupling, so integrating the learned ODE from $p_0$ pushes mass toward~$p_1$.

\paragraph{Conditional flow matching.}
In the setting of this note, the target is conditional on the stimulus $\theta$.
One trains $v_{\phi}(x,t\mid\theta)$ by replacing $p_1$ with $p(\cdot\mid\theta)$ in the expectation over~$x_1$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CFM}}(\phi)
  =
  \mathbb{E}_{\theta}\,
  \mathbb{E}_{t \sim \mathcal{U}[0,1]}\,
  \mathbb{E}_{x_0 \sim p_0,\; x_1 \sim p(\cdot\mid\theta)}
  \Bigl[
    \bigl\lVert v_{\phi}(x_t,t\mid\theta) - u_t \bigr\rVert_2^2
  \Bigr],
\end{equation}
with $x_t$ as in \eqref{eq:flow_affine_bridge} and $u_t$ as in \eqref{eq:flow_affine_velocity} along each sampled bridge (we use the same symbols for both objectives). In this note, we used affine path with cosine scheduler and used a MLP to parameterize the velocity field, see Supplementary information.

\subsection{Estimating the score from flow matching}

The result of this subection is included in Yaron Lipman \emph{et al.}, \emph{Flow Matching Guide and Code} (preprint, 2024), \url{https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2412.06264} (equation~4.78). The following is a self-contained derivation.

Denoising score matching learns the score function $\nabla_x \log p(x)$ directly (or a noisy version of it). Flow matching, by contrast, learns a velocity field. For Gaussian probability paths, these two quantities are closely related: the score can be recovered from the velocity by a simple analytic transformation.

Consider the Gaussian path
\begin{equation}
  x_t = \beta(t)\,x_1 + \alpha(t)\,\varepsilon,
  \qquad
  \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I),
  \label{eq:gaussian_prob_path}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha(t)$ and $\beta(t)$ are scalar schedules. This Gaussian path is the affine path \eqref{eq:flow_affine_bridge} when the base draw $x_0$ is isotropic normal. Conditionally on $x_1$, this defines a Gaussian perturbation of the data. Averaging over $x_1$ gives a noisy marginal density $p_t(x_t)$. Our ultimate goal is the data score $\nabla_x \log p(x)$, but when $t$ is close to $1$, the noisy density $p_t$ is close to the data density $p$, so it is often enough to estimate the noisy score
\[
s_t(x_t) := \nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t),
\]
which converges to the data score as $t\to 1$.

The key observation is that, under the Gaussian path, the conditional score has a very simple form (see Subsection~\ref{subsec:si-gaussian-conditional-score}):
\begin{equation}
  s_t(x_t \mid x_1)
  =
  -\frac{x_t-\beta(t)x_1}{\alpha(t)^2}
  =
  -\frac{\varepsilon}{\alpha(t)}.
  \label{eq:conditional_score_simple}
\end{equation}
Taking conditional expectation over $x_1$ given $x_t$ yields
\begin{equation}
  s_t(x_t)
  =
  -\frac{1}{\alpha(t)}\,\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon \mid x_t].
  \label{eq:score_from_eps_mean}
\end{equation}
Therefore, estimating the noisy score reduces to estimating the posterior mean of the (unknown) Gaussian noise.

To connect this quantity to flow matching, we differentiate the path \eqref{eq:gaussian_prob_path} with respect to time. This gives the conditional velocity
\[
u_t = \dot{\beta}(t)\,x_1 + \dot{\alpha}(t)\,\varepsilon.
\]
Now combine this expression with the path equation itself. Since both involve the same unknowns, $x_1$ and $\varepsilon$, we can eliminate $x_1$ and solve for $\varepsilon$ in terms of $x_t$ and $u_t$. This gives
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon
  =
  \frac{
    u_t - \frac{\dot{\beta}(t)}{\beta(t)}x_t
  }{
    \dot{\alpha}(t)-\alpha(t)\frac{\dot{\beta}(t)}{\beta(t)}
  }.
  \label{eq:eps_from_velocity_short}
\end{equation}

Finally, take conditional expectation given $x_t$. If we define the marginal velocity field by
\[
v_t(x_t) := \mathbb{E}[u_t \mid x_t],
\]
then \eqref{eq:eps_from_velocity_short} immediately implies
\[
\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon \mid x_t]
=
\frac{
  v_t(x_t) - \frac{\dot{\beta}(t)}{\beta(t)}x_t
}{
  \dot{\alpha}(t)-\alpha(t)\frac{\dot{\beta}(t)}{\beta(t)}
}.
\]
Substituting this into \eqref{eq:score_from_eps_mean}, we obtain the noisy score in terms of the velocity field:
\begin{equation}
  s_t(x_t)
  =
  \frac{
    \frac{\dot{\beta}(t)}{\beta(t)}x_t - v_t(x_t)
  }{
    \alpha(t)\left(
      \dot{\alpha}(t)-\alpha(t)\frac{\dot{\beta}(t)}{\beta(t)}
    \right)
  }.
  \label{eq:score_from_velocity_clean}
\end{equation}

Finally, a well-trained flow matching velocity network $v_\phi(x_t,t)$ learns the marginal velocity field $v_t(x_t)$. By replacing $v_t$ with $v_\phi(x_t,t)$, we are able to compute the noisy score $s_t(x_t)$ which is an approximation of the data score. Since $x_1$ is given in the data rather than $x_t$, therefore the score we in practice computed is $s_t(x_1)$ where we fix $t = 0.8$ in this note.

  \item Can we interpret the choice of $t$ value as adding noise to data and measures the robustness of representation to noise attack?

\subsection{Supplementary information: velocity network in flow matching.}
In the experiments reported here, the conditional (posterior) velocity is parameterized as a fully connected network (MLP): the input is $(\theta_t, x, \psi(t)) \in \mathbb{R}^{1 + d + 1}$, where $\theta_t$ is the scalar state along the flow bridge, $x \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ is the observation, and $\psi(t)=\log t - \log(1-t)$ is a logit-time feature (with $t$ restricted to a compact subinterval of $(0,1)$ so that $\psi$ is finite).
The map consists of three hidden layers of width $128$ with SiLU activations, followed by a linear readout to a scalar velocity.
The prior velocity uses the same MLP pattern on $(\theta_t, \psi(t)) \in \mathbb{R}^{2}$ with hidden width $128$ and three hidden layers.

\subsection{Supplementary information: cosine scheduler in flow matching.}
With $t \in [0,1]$, the cosine choice for the affine path \eqref{eq:flow_affine_bridge} is
\begin{equation}
  \alpha(t) = \cos\!\Bigl(\frac{\pi}{2}\,t\Bigr),
  \qquad
  \beta(t) = \sin\!\Bigl(\frac{\pi}{2}\,t\Bigr).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Supplementary information: conditional score under the Gaussian path}
\label{subsec:si-gaussian-conditional-score}

We consider the Gaussian probability path
\begin{equation}
  x_t = \beta(t)\,x_1 + \alpha(t)\,\varepsilon,
  \qquad
  \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I).
  \label{eq:gaussian_prob_path_appendix}
\end{equation}
Conditioned on $x_1$, the randomness comes only from $\varepsilon$, so $x_t \mid x_1$ is Gaussian with mean $\beta(t)x_1$ and covariance $\alpha(t)^2 I$:
\begin{equation}
  p_t(x_t \mid x_1)
  =
  \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(x_t;\beta(t)x_1,\alpha(t)^2 I\bigr).
\end{equation}
Therefore,
\begin{equation}
  \log p_t(x_t \mid x_1)
  =
  -\frac{d}{2}\log\!\bigl(2\pi \alpha(t)^2\bigr)
  -\frac{1}{2\alpha(t)^2}\,
  \|x_t-\beta(t)x_1\|^2,
\end{equation}
where $d$ is the dimension of $x_t$. Since the first term does not depend on $x_t$, differentiating with respect to $x_t$ gives
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t \mid x_1)
  =
  -\frac{1}{2\alpha(t)^2}\,
  \nabla_{x_t}\|x_t-\beta(t)x_1\|^2.
\end{equation}
Using the identity
\[
\nabla_x \|x-a\|^2 = 2(x-a),
\]
we obtain
\begin{equation}
  s_t(x_t \mid x_1)
  :=
  \nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t \mid x_1)
  =
  -\frac{x_t-\beta(t)x_1}{\alpha(t)^2}.
  \label{eq:conditional_score_first_form}
\end{equation}

Finally, from the path definition \eqref{eq:gaussian_prob_path_appendix},
\[
x_t-\beta(t)x_1=\alpha(t)\varepsilon.
\]
Substituting this into \eqref{eq:conditional_score_first_form} yields
\begin{equation}
  s_t(x_t \mid x_1)
  =
  -\frac{\alpha(t)\varepsilon}{\alpha(t)^2}
  =
  -\frac{\varepsilon}{\alpha(t)}.
  \label{eq:conditional_score_simple_appendix}
\end{equation}

Thus, under the Gaussian path, the conditional score has a particularly simple interpretation: it is just the negative latent noise, rescaled by the noise level $\alpha(t)$.

\subsection{Supplementary information: the marginal score as the expectation of the conditional score}

We now explain why the noisy marginal score
\[
s_t(x_t) := \nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t)
\]
can be written as the conditional expectation of the conditional score
\[
s_t(x_t\mid x_1) := \nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t\mid x_1).
\]

Recall that the noisy marginal density is obtained by averaging the conditional density over the data distribution:
\begin{equation}
  p_t(x_t)
  =
  \int p_t(x_t\mid x_1)\,p(x_1)\,dx_1.
  \label{eq:marginal_density}
\end{equation}
Differentiating both sides with respect to $x_t$ gives
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_{x_t} p_t(x_t)
  =
  \int \nabla_{x_t} p_t(x_t\mid x_1)\,p(x_1)\,dx_1.
\end{equation}
Using the identity
\[
\nabla_{x_t} p_t(x_t\mid x_1)
=
p_t(x_t\mid x_1)\,\nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t\mid x_1),
\]
we obtain
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_{x_t} p_t(x_t)
  =
  \int p_t(x_t\mid x_1)\,p(x_1)\,
  \nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t\mid x_1)\,dx_1.
\end{equation}
Now divide both sides by $p_t(x_t)$. By definition of the score,
\begin{equation}
  s_t(x_t)
  =
  \nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t)
  =
  \frac{1}{p_t(x_t)}
  \int p_t(x_t\mid x_1)\,p(x_1)\,
  \nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t\mid x_1)\,dx_1.
\end{equation}
The factor
\[
\frac{p_t(x_t\mid x_1)\,p(x_1)}{p_t(x_t)}
\]
is exactly the posterior density $p(x_1\mid x_t)$. Therefore,
\begin{equation}
  s_t(x_t)
  =
  \int p(x_1\mid x_t)\,s_t(x_t\mid x_1)\,dx_1
  =
  \mathbb{E}\!\left[s_t(x_t\mid x_1)\mid x_t\right].
  \label{eq:marginal_score_as_expectation}
\end{equation}
